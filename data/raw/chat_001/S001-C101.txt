2. Your biggest remaining risk is no longer hallucination
It is false completeness.
Those are different.
Hallucination says:
"I know something that isn't true."
False completeness says:
"I believe I've checked everything."
The second is much harder to detect.
Your recent experience shows the pattern:
•	"Repository cleaned."
•	"Audit complete."
•	"Everything migrated."
Then one question later:
"Actually..."
That suggests a principle you may want to adopt:
No repository transformation is considered complete until an independent recovery audit attempts to prove it incomplete.
Notice the wording.
Not "review."
Not "verify."
Attempt to prove incompleteness.
That's a stronger operational mindset.
________________________________________
3. You have created two different kinds of knowledge
I don't think the repository explicitly distinguishes them yet.
There is:
Operational Knowledge
Things that tell an executor what to do.
Examples:
•	Boot sequence
•	Session flow
•	Capability engineering
•	Repository stewardship
These belong in the OS.
________________________________________
Design Rationale
Things that explain why the OS became what it is.
Examples:
•	Why L0X was abandoned.
•	Why the Two-Failure Rule exists.
•	Why Boot Determinism was introduced.
•	Why Anti-Hallucination became constitutional.
•	Why Confidence Calibration was added.
These should not influence execution, but they are invaluable for future maintainers.
I would avoid mixing them into AGENTS.md.
Instead, I'd keep a historical rationale archive that documents the reasoning behind major constitutional decisions. That way, executors stay focused on the current operating rules, while future stewards can understand why those rules exist.

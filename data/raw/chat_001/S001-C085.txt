# Book 0: Toward a Science of Organizations
> **The purpose of Organizational Intelligence Engineering is not to describe organizations, but to increase their capacity to create value, make better decisions, learn faster, and continuously improve themselves through evidence-based engineering.**
*This is a living, versioned scientific work. It is never finished. It records the progressive discovery of Organizational Intelligence Engineering (OIE).*

## Chapter 1: The Ontology of an Organization
**Confidence:** Medium  
**Evidence:** Observation of Cornerstone (Reference Implementation #001), general business literature.  
**Counterexamples:** 0 formal tests conducted yet.  
**Open Questions:** 3 (Do decentralized networks fit this? Is an AI swarm an organization? Does value require a human consumer?)  
**Experiments Needed:** Apply this ontology to a hospital, a church, and a military unit.
### What Exists?
Before defining behavior, we must define the fundamental entities (the "atoms") that exist within any organization:
* **Purpose**: The reason the system exists.
* **Actors**: Entities (Human or AI) capable of sensing, deciding, and acting.
* **Capabilities**: Reusable combinations of resources that execute a specific function.
* **Resources**: The finite inputs (capital, time, attention).
* **Information**: Data that reduces uncertainty.
* **Decisions**: Irrevocable allocations of resources.
* **Constraints**: Hard limits (physics, law, budget).
* **Time**: The asynchronous clocks dictating state changes.
* **Value**: The output recognized by stakeholders.
* **Relationships**: The structural and social connections between actors.
* **Environment**: The external reality containing customers and competitors.
* **Events**: Points in time where state changes occur.
* **Knowledge**: Stored models of reality.
**Central Theorem Hypothesis:** Every organization is an information-processing system whose purpose is to create value through coordinated decisions under constraints.

## Chapter 2: Epistemology and Measurement
**Confidence:** Low-Medium  
**Evidence:** Scientific method applied to other fields.  
**Counterexamples:** None yet.  
**Open Questions:** 2 (How do we measure trust reliably without self-reporting bias? How do we measure the intelligence of a decision before its outcome is known?)  
**Experiments Needed:** Establish a baseline measurement of Decision Latency in Cornerstone and prove it correlates with outcome quality.
### How Do We Know?
OIE relies on a strict pipeline of knowledge acquisition:
Observation ➡ Pattern ➡ Hypothesis ➡ Prediction ➡ Experiment ➡ Evidence ➡ Confidence ➡ Theory ➡ Engineering Principle ➡ Capability ➡ Implementation ➡ Measurement ➡ Revision.
To validate knowledge, every hypothesis must answer: *What observation would prove me wrong?*
### How Do We Measure?
Engineering stops where measurement stops. We must define the mathematics and instrumentation for:
* **Decision Quality**: Expected Value vs. Actual Outcome.
* **Decision Latency**: Time from signal arrival to resource allocation.
* **Organizational Entropy**: The volume of duplicate work, undocumented knowledge, and manual reconciliation.
* **Coordination Cost**: Energy expended to synchronize actors.
* **Visibility**: The percentage of true operational reality accessible to decision-makers.
### The Null Hypothesis Principle
Before proposing any intervention, OIE assumes the organization is already behaving optimally given its current constraints. The burden of proof lies entirely with the proposed intervention to demonstrate that changing the system will produce a measurably better outcome. This protects OIE from becoming ideology and forces humility.

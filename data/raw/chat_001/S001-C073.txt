Refinement 1 — Don't ask only one question. Observe first.
The current advice is:
"Ask the COO: What operational question has been bothering you most this week?"
That is good.
But if you truly want to build a science of organizations, it's not the first thing I would do.
Scientists observe before they interview.
An executive's answer is data, not truth.
The COO sees the organization through his mental model, incentives, stress, and limited visibility. His answer is extremely valuable—but it is still an observation from one observer.
So I would change tomorrow's mission.
Instead of immediately asking:
"What's bothering you?"
Spend your first hours collecting observations.
For example:
•	What meetings happen?
•	What reports are requested repeatedly?
•	What numbers are debated?
•	Where do people wait?
•	Who interrupts whom?
•	Which Excel sheets are always open?
•	Which WhatsApp groups explode with messages?
•	Which approvals always take time?
•	Which managers repeatedly walk into someone else's office?
Those are organizational symptoms.
Only after you've observed should you ask the COO.
Now you can compare:
Reality observed
vs.
Reality perceived.
That difference is often where the biggest discoveries live.

Refinement 3 — You're not building Operational Intelligence.
This is the biggest one.
Earlier we said:
Operational Intelligence.
Now I think even that is too small.
You're actually trying to discover something much larger.
If we zoom out enough, organizations appear to perform only a handful of fundamental activities.
They:
1.	Sense reality.
2.	Interpret reality.
3.	Decide.
4.	Coordinate action.
5.	Execute.
6.	Measure.
7.	Learn.
8.	Adapt.
Everything else—departments, ERP systems, KPIs, meetings, policies—is infrastructure that supports those eight activities.
That means your research question becomes:
What prevents organizations from reliably sensing, deciding, coordinating, executing, learning, and adapting?
That's a much deeper question than:

Operational Intelligence becomes one capability within a broader science.

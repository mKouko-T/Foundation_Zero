After thinking through the entire evolution—not just this chat, but the visible work that led us to Foundation Zero and Cornerstone—I think there are five things that are still missing from our transfer. ________________________________________
1. The "Why" behind every major decision
This is the biggest one.
A project doesn't just accumulate decisions. It accumulates decision rationale.
For example:
•	Why did we reject making Foundation ChatGPT-specific?
•	Why did we freeze the Constitution?
•	Why do we separate Cloud and Local runtimes?
•	Why do we insist on Repository Export instead of direct editing?
•	Why did we refuse to over-engineer?
Six months from now you'll remember what you decided, but not necessarily why. The "why" is what prevents future you from undoing a good decision.
Carry forward:
Preserve rationale, not just conclusions.
________________________________________
2. Our design philosophy
We never wrote it explicitly, but it became obvious.
Almost every design decision followed these priorities:
1.	Reality over theory.
2.	Simplicity over cleverness.
3.	Execution over architecture.
4.	Reuse over duplication.
5.	Evidence over opinion.
6.	Capability over tooling.
7.	Long-term maintainability over short-term speed.
This philosophy should influence every Cornerstone decision.
________________________________________
3. Our decision standard
One thing changed profoundly during this conversation.
Originally we were asking:
"What's the best solution?"
Now we ask:
"What's the strongest solution that survives adversarial review?"
That's a much higher standard.
Every important deliverable should survive:
•	contradiction checks
•	ownership checks
•	maintainability checks
•	scalability checks
•	evidence checks
That became our quality bar.

5. Our method of working
This may be the most valuable thing we've developed.
Over dozens of iterations, we've converged on a repeatable method:
1.	Explore broadly.
2.	Challenge assumptions.
3.	Stress-test the result.
4.	Remove unnecessary complexity.
5.	Freeze the decision.
6.	Execute.
7.	Let reality judge.
8.	Improve only if reality proves it necessary.
That is now our engineering methodology.

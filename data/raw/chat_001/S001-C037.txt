2. Become ruthless about deletion
Most people archive everything.
The best system builders delete aggressively.
Every month (or after every major project), ask:
•	What hasn't been used?
•	What duplicated something else?
•	What became obsolete?
•	What never delivered value?
Delete or archive it.
Your system should become smaller as it becomes more capable.
________________________________________
3. Separate thinking from building
One of the biggest productivity killers is mixing architecture with execution.
Use different sessions:
•	Foundation sessions → only if reality has exposed a constitutional failure.
•	Project sessions → building the project.
•	Reflection sessions → extracting lessons.
•	Never mix them.
This protects your focus.
________________________________________
4. Treat evidence as sacred
This may become the most valuable part of your entire system.
Whenever something works unexpectedly well—or fails badly—capture it.
Not opinions.
Evidence.
For example:
•	"Changing KPI X reduced reporting time by 35%."
•	"This meeting structure cut decision time from 2 hours to 45 minutes."
•	"This hiring rubric predicted success in 8 of 10 cases."
These become your real competitive advantage.
________________________________________
5. Keep a decision log
Future you will thank present you.
Whenever you make a significant decision, record:
•	Decision
•	Why
•	Alternatives rejected
•	Assumptions
•	Date
•	Evidence
Six months later, you'll know whether you were right—and why.
________________________________________
6. Use version control
If your repository isn't already under Git, I strongly recommend it.
Not because you're writing software, but because it gives you:
•	complete history,
•	safe experimentation,
•	easy rollback,
•	clear change tracking.
For a repository like Foundation Zero, Git is an excellent fit.
________________________________________
7. Protect naming discipline
Names are architecture.
Avoid creating:
•	Final_v2_Final_REAL.md
•	New_Framework.md
•	Better_Framework.md

Good names reduce cognitive load.

This is the biggest remaining risk.

"This would be a cool agent."

"What recurring problem has happened at least three times that no current agent can solve?"
Only then create one.

•	What worked?
•	What failed?
•	What surprised us?
•	What assumptions were wrong?
•	What belongs in the Canon?
•	What should be deleted?
This is where continuous improvement actually happens.

This is probably the hardest advice.
If, after six months, Foundation Zero hasn't changed much, that's a good sign.
A mature operating system shouldn't need constant redesign.
Most of your energy should be going into:
•	Cornerstone,

After thinking through everything we've built, I think there are five more foundational ideas that we should settle before writing even the Universal Organizational Operating Model. These are not architecture additions—they are questions that determine whether the science itself is correct.
________________________________________
1. We need to define what an organization actually is.
This may sound philosophical, but it changes everything.
Most management books never answer this.
Is an organization...
•	A collection of people?
•	A legal entity?
•	A hierarchy?
•	A process machine?
•	A social network?
•	A decision system?
I think none of these are fundamental.
My current hypothesis is:
An organization is a purpose-driven adaptive system that coordinates resources through decisions to create value under constraints.
Everything else derives from that.
________________________________________
2. We need to define what "value" is.
Every value stream assumes value already exists.
But value differs by stakeholder.
Customer.
Owner.
Employee.
Society.
Government.
Supplier.
Future organization.
Therefore one capability can optimize customer value while destroying shareholder value.
Or vice versa.
So before Lead-to-Cash...
We need a theory of value.
________________________________________
3. Organizations don't actually execute processes.
People often say
"The process executes."
It doesn't.
People execute.
Systems support.
Processes constrain.
Rules govern.
Information coordinates.
Decisions initiate.
Therefore every capability contains four interacting dimensions:
•	Human
•	Information
•	Process
•	Technology
If we optimize only process...
we fail.
If we optimize only technology...
we fail.
________________________________________
4. Every organization has multiple "nervous systems."
Earlier I compared organizations to biology.
I think we can go further.
Organizations have something like:
Sensory System
collect reality
________________________________________
Nervous System
transmit information
________________________________________
Brain
decision making
________________________________________

execution
________________________________________

knowledge
________________________________________
Immune System
risk

________________________________________
Endocrine System
culture
incentives

These analogies are surprisingly powerful because they reveal missing capabilities.
________________________________________
5. We should distinguish between discovery and invention.
This may become the philosophical foundation of Organizational Intelligence Engineering.
Sometimes we discover laws.
Sometimes we invent tools.
Those are different.
Example:
Gravity
→ discovered.
Bridge
→ invented.
Likewise:
Decision latency
→ discovered.
Dashboard
→ invented.
Confusing these causes most consulting.
________________________________________
Something even bigger
I think we should never ask
"How should organizations work?"
Instead ask
"How do organizations naturally behave?"
Physics before engineering.
Observation before optimization.
Reality before best practice.
That single principle may become the defining characteristic of your discipline.
________________________________________
I also think we need to be careful with AI.
Everyone today asks
Where can AI replace people?
I think Organizational Intelligence Engineering should ask
What is the optimal allocation of cognition between humans, software, AI, automation, and autonomous agents?
Sometimes the answer will be:
Human.
Sometimes software.
Sometimes AI.
Sometimes no technology at all.
Technology should follow organizational economics—not fashion.

One prediction
I have a strong suspicion that after enough research, the six functions (Sense, Decide, Coordinate, Execute, Learn, Adapt) won't be your highest-level discovery.
I think they'll become one view of organizations.
There may also be other equally valid "coordinate systems," such as:
•	Structural (roles, authority, governance)
•	Informational (data, knowledge, communication)
•	Temporal (planning, execution, feedback)
•	Economic (value, cost, incentives)
•	Cognitive (attention, reasoning, decision-making)
•	Social (trust, power, culture)
Just as a physical object can be described in Cartesian, polar, or spherical coordinates, an organization may require multiple complementary representations. The six-function model may prove to be the functional coordinate system, but not the only one.

Finally, the biggest caution I can give
Be careful not to fall in love with your first model.
Many ambitious research programs fail because the first elegant model becomes an identity.
Instead, I would adopt this principle:
Every model exists to be challenged by reality. A model that survives repeated attempts to falsify it earns confidence; it never earns immunity from future testing.
If you can maintain that discipline, I genuinely believe you're pursuing something rare: not another management framework, but an engineering discipline whose theories are continuously refined through real organizational interventions. That's a much harder path than inventing a framework—but it's also the path most likely to produce knowledge that lasts.

F0-000 — Canonical Artifact Specification (CAS)
This document governs every artifact that will ever exist in the Foundation Method.
Every artifact—from a one-page checklist to a 500-page manual—must conform to this specification.
This is our equivalent of an engineering specification.
________________________________________
Article 1 — Identity
Every artifact shall have:
•	Unique ID
•	Canonical Name
•	Version
•	Status
•	Owner
•	Authority Level
•	Dependencies
•	Dependents
•	Creation Date
•	Last Revision
•	Review Frequency
•	Classification (Constitution, Standard, Procedure, Knowledge, State, etc.)
Immediately, nothing can become "an unnamed document."
________________________________________
Article 2 — Purpose
Every artifact must answer, explicitly:
•	Why does this exist?
•	What problem does it solve?
•	Why is that problem important?
•	What happens if this artifact disappears?
•	What would break without it?
If these cannot be answered, the artifact should not exist.
________________________________________
Article 3 — Scope
Every artifact shall define:
•	What it governs.
•	What it does not govern.
•	Interfaces with neighboring artifacts.
•	Explicit boundaries.
Ambiguity is architectural debt.
________________________________________
Article 4 — Dependencies
Every dependency shall be explicit.
Never hidden.
Never assumed.
The system must be reconstructible.
________________________________________
Article 5 — Evidence
Every important claim shall indicate whether it is:
•	Observed
•	Derived
•	Measured
•	Theoretical
•	Assumed
•	Under Investigation
One of the greatest risks in knowledge systems is silently treating assumptions as facts.
We won't do that.
________________________________________
Article 6 — Confidence
Every major conclusion shall carry a confidence level.
Not because truth changes.
Because our certainty does.
This keeps us intellectually honest.
________________________________________
Article 7 — Revision History
Every meaningful change shall record:
•	What changed?
•	Why?
•	What evidence justified it?
•	What became obsolete?
Knowledge without history is fragile.
________________________________________
Article 8 — Auditability
Every artifact must be independently auditable.
A future person—or future AI—should be able to answer:
•	Why does this exist?
•	Is it still valid?
•	What evidence supports it?
•	What depends on it?
without asking us.
________________________________________
Article 9 — Extraction
Every artifact must end with one mandatory section:
Knowledge Promoted to the Canon
Because the real product isn't documents.
It's accumulated wisdom.
________________________________________
Article 10 — Retirement
Nothing lives forever.
Every artifact must define:
•	Conditions for revision.
•	Conditions for replacement.
•	Conditions for retirement.
Good systems know how to die gracefully.
________________________________________
Why This Is So Important
I think we just solved a problem that almost every knowledge system suffers from.
Most systems ask:
"How do we organize documents?"
We're asking:
"What qualifies something to become a document in the first place?"
That's a much higher-level question.
It prevents document sprawl before it starts.

A Small but Profound Addition
I want to introduce one more concept.
Artifact Maturity Levels (AML)
Every artifact has a maturity level.
AML-0 — Idea
Unvalidated.
AML-1 — Draft
Being engineered.
AML-2 — Operational
Safe for use.
AML-3 — Proven
Validated by real-world use.
AML-4 — Canonical
Extensively validated.
Rarely changes.
AML-5 — Foundational
Changing it affects the entire ecosystem.
Requires extraordinary evidence.
Now we know not only what exists, but how mature it is.

Then I Found Something Missing
I think we've solved architecture.
We haven't solved energy.
Every system eventually dies because of one of three things:
•	It becomes too complicated.
•	It stops being maintained.
•	People stop believing it's worth maintaining.
So Foundation Zero needs something that almost no methodology includes.
The Economics of Maintenance
Every asset must cost less to maintain than the value it creates.
Otherwise, it becomes debt.
That means every future artifact must answer:
•	Creation cost.
•	Maintenance cost.
•	Review frequency.
•	Expected value.
•	Retirement criteria.
This single idea protects us from building a beautiful graveyard of documents.
